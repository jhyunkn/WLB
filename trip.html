July 10–18 · NYC → San Francisco loop

California parks loop.

A 9-day rental-car route from San Francisco through Sequoia, Death Valley, Yosemite, and back to San Francisco. The plan balances iconic scenery with realistic driving days, summer heat limits, and national park congestion.

Strategy

This is not a maximum-mileage itinerary. It is a controlled loop: long drives are placed between lodge bases, Death Valley is handled at low-risk hours, and Yosemite gets two nights so it does not become a rushed pass-through.

Route logic

SF → Sequoia → Death Valley → East Sierra → Yosemite → SF

The cleanest structure is to enter the Sierra from the west, cross south and east toward Death Valley, recover north along US-395, then enter Yosemite through Tioga Pass if open.

Heat logic

Death Valley is sunrise, sunset, and car-based.

In July, avoid lower-elevation hikes. Use Furnace Creek / Stovepipe as a short scenic base, then leave before the extreme afternoon heat.

Crowd logic

Yosemite needs early starts.

No season-wide timed-entry reservation is planned for 2026, but parking and congestion still shape the day. Treat 6:00–7:00 AM arrival as the operating rule.

Lodging plan

Book by geography first, then quality. The wrong lodging location can turn a good route into a daily driving penalty.

7/10

San Francisco

Use SF airport area if your flight lands late. Use Marina / Union Square / SOMA if you want one city evening. Goal: easy start next morning.

7/11–7/12

Sequoia base

Priority order: Wuksachi Lodge, Three Rivers, then Visalia. Staying inside/near the park saves time and protects your General Sherman morning.

7/13

Death Valley

Best: The Oasis / Ranch at Death Valley or Stovepipe Wells. Practical fallback: Pahrump, but that changes the flow and adds driving.

7/14

Eastern Sierra

Mammoth Lakes is more comfortable and has more services. Lee Vining is strategically closer to Tioga Pass and Yosemite's east entrance.

7/15–7/16

Yosemite base

Priority order: Yosemite Valley lodging, El Portal, Wawona/Tenaya, Mariposa, Groveland. Closer is worth paying for in July.

7/17

San Francisco / SFO

Stay near SFO if your 7/18 flight is early. Stay in SF proper if your flight is later and you want one final city night.

Buy the America the Beautiful pass.

For a multi-park route, the $80 interagency annual pass is cleaner than paying separate private-vehicle fees at each national park. It also simplifies re-entry and unexpected park adjustments.

Fee logic: Yosemite and Sequoia/Kings Canyon private-vehicle passes are each $35, Death Valley is $30, and the interagency annual pass is $80 for U.S. citizens/residents.

Booking checklist

Sequence matters. Secure the scarce pieces first, then optimize the rest.

1

Flights

Book NYC → SFO for July 10 and SFO → NYC for July 18. Morning/early afternoon arrival on July 10 makes the next day easier.

2

Lodging

Book Yosemite and Sequoia first. Then Death Valley. Then SF and Eastern Sierra. Park-adjacent lodging is the scarce resource.

3

Car

Reserve a comfortable fuel-efficient car or compact SUV. Avoid low-clearance anxiety, but do not rely on off-road travel in a rental.

4

Road checks

Before each mountain/desert segment, check NPS and Caltrans for closures, construction, smoke, flooding, and Tioga Pass status.

5

Supplies

Water case, electrolyte packets, sunscreen, hats, light layers, cooler, offline maps, power bank, and printed lodging addresses.

6

Operating rules

Start early. Do not hike Death Valley low elevations in heat. Treat Yosemite parking as a morning task. Build buffers into every long drive.

Critical notes

Yosemite entry

For 2026, Yosemite announced it will not use a season-wide timed reservation system. This helps flexibility, but not parking. Arrive early, especially July 15–16.

Sequoia conditions

Summer conditions vary by elevation. Lower foothills can be hot while the groves are cooler. Parking at Giant Forest and General Sherman can fill early.

Death Valley safety

July can be extreme. Keep the visit short, scenic, and early/late. Carry at least one gallon of water per person per day and avoid hot lower-elevation hikes.
